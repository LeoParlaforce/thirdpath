## Elaboration as a Duty

There is a duty, for every human subject, to elaborate what they live through. It is even what distinguishes the human from everything that is not: not merely undergoing what happens, but doing something with it. Putting words to it. Establishing connections. Understanding, as far as possible, what is at play. Speech, thought, inner work on one's own history — that is what makes, properly speaking, a subject.

To say of something that concerns us *"it's just how it is, there's nothing to say about it"* is, on these grounds, unacceptable. It is to renounce the bare minimum of what is human in us. And yet, this position is becoming widespread — particularly when it comes to questions touching on desire, sexuality, identity. We are told that certain things are *given*, that they do not have to be questioned, that we must accept them as such, and that any attempt to think them would be a form of violence.

Homosexuality is today one of the topics most exposed to this lockdown. It is treated as an untouchable given. It can be spoken of in defense, in celebration, in self-affirmation — but not thought clinically, not interrogated as to what it articulates for this or that subject, not questioned in any way that would not be an affirmation. This prohibition is not innocent. It dispossesses the subjects concerned of what could precisely free them: access to their own truth.

This article proposes to examine that lockdown, and to set out, from a psychoanalytic standpoint, what can be said seriously about homosexuality — including in its possible relation to perversion, which is precisely one of the questions that has become impossible to raise elsewhere. Not to accuse. To think.

## "I Have a Right to Suffer, It's the Other Who Makes Me Suffer"

Much of the contemporary difficulty in speaking about these questions comes from a massive shift in the way subjects speak about their suffering. The phrase that comes back everywhere, in a thousand forms, is: *no, I have a right to suffer, because it's the other who makes me suffer. I would be fine if it weren't for…* And then the subject names the context: capitalism, patriarchy, society, the system, norms, their family, their past.

This formulation is more devious than it appears. To say *I have a right to suffer* is to introduce a value judgment about suffering — there would therefore be people who suffer *wrongly*, as opposed to those who suffer *rightly*. This distinction is extremely violent. It ranks subjects according to the legitimacy of their pain. It is, incidentally, precisely the kind of thing that is worked on in therapy — because it is rarely the subject who thinks in these terms who comes out best.

And above all, to claim that one would be fine *if it weren't for* the environment is to imagine that one would exist outside one's own context. This idea is a great poverty of thought. The subject is always taken up in a context — familial, social, historical, linguistic. The context is not a superficial layer that could be subtracted to find a "pure self" that would be fine. The subject *is* their way of arranging themselves with what has happened to them. There is no subject under the context, just as there is no core under the leaves of an onion.

Therapy does not consist of counting up wrongs. Therapy is always *therapy of the relation to the other*. The question is not "whose fault is it" — that question has no way out. The question is: *what can we make of it?* To suffer is to undergo. The analytic position consists in not contenting oneself with undergoing, in taking back the helm of what is at play, in knowing what we do with what happens to us, even when we did not choose it.

## "Born That Way": Biologism as the New Obscurantism

Contemporary discourse on homosexuality relies on a formula it takes for the height of benevolence: *they were born that way, they can't help it*. This formula seems harmless. It is not. Beneath its progressive appearance, it carries out an operation that deserves to be named for what it is.

**To claim that a position of the subject is entirely determined by their biology is obscurantism.** It is totalitarian thought in its most ancient version — the one that believed one could read a person's character in the shape of their skull, the color of their skin, their blood. That this biologism takes today the form of a benevolent defense of minorities rather than a racial hierarchy changes nothing about its structure. It is the same operation: reducing the subject to a biological substrate presumed to carry their truth, and cutting away everything in them that belongs to the symbolic, to history, to desire, to speech.

**One is not born homosexual. One becomes it — and even, one chooses it.** This sentence scandalizes, and one must understand why.

It scandalizes because contemporary subjects hear "to choose" in the sense of conscious and deliberate. For them, "I chose" means "I willed," that is, "I could have willed otherwise if I had wanted to." From that point of view, to tell someone they chose their homosexuality amounts to asking them why they don't reverse course, why they didn't choose differently. This is intolerable. And they are right on one point: they did not choose consciously. No one wakes up one morning deliberating about their sexual orientation the way one deliberates about a purchase.

But they are wrong about the rest — and their error is to have wanted to banish the unconscious from thought. They believe the subject is what they know of themselves, what they can formulate in the first person singular. They believe that to choose is to choose with one's consciousness. And since this idea does not hold up the moment one actually looks at the psychic life of a human being, they suffer from it — without being able to name what makes them suffer, because they have mutilated in advance the tool that would have allowed them to.

The subject is for the most part unconscious. They choose, constantly, things they do not know they are choosing. They take positions, they direct their desire, they reject this object and invest that one, and all of this operates in a register to which their consciousness has only indirect and belated access. A choice can perfectly well be unconscious, and it is no less their choice for that — it is even far more their choice than the rationalizations they then deploy to explain to themselves what is happening to them.

What the defenders of *born that way* refuse is not responsibility in the moral sense. It is responsibility in the analytic sense: the idea that they are the author, even unwittingly, of what happens to them. They would like life to spare them their choices. *I didn't choose, I chose nothing, it fell upon me.* And it is precisely this position that makes therapy impossible — because for them, therapy would consist of *learning to live as one is*, that is, of not touching anything. Whereas therapy is the opposite: it is giving the subject access to what they have chosen without knowing it, so that they can do something with it.

To say that homosexuality is an unconscious choice is neither to pathologize it nor to condemn it. It is to give the homosexual subject what contemporary right-thinking takes from them: the dignity of having a destiny that thought can illuminate.

## The Denial Betrays Itself in Its Paradoxes

Contemporary discourse on homosexuality holds simultaneously two statements that cannot stand together. *It is not a symptom, it is not a pathology*, it says. And in the same breath: *recognize the singularity of my journey, my suffering, what I have been through.*

But what is called "the singularity of a journey" is exactly what clinical practice calls a **symptom** — in the psychoanalytic sense, that is, a subjective formation that condenses a history and a truth. The subject wants to be recognized for their symptom *and* demands that no one touch it. They ask for the dignity of having a destiny and the exemption from the work of thinking it.

It is the paradoxes of discourse that betray the repression. The subject does not really want to be unaware of what they repress — they would *prefer* to be unaware of it. Psychoanalytic denial always rests on bad faith: one knows, but one does not want to know that one knows. And this repressed knowledge returns in the contradictions of discourse, in the obligatory turns that do not hold up the moment one looks at them.

## What Freud Did — and Why It Took Courage to Do It

Many today describe Freud as a misogynist, a latent homophobe, a man of his century caught in prejudice. The exact opposite is true. For Freud's relation to women, see our article [Was Freud a Misogynist?](/articles/was-freud-a-misogynist).

In his famous letter to the American mother who questioned him about her son, in April 1935, Freud writes that homosexuality is neither an advantage, nor a shame, nor a vice, nor a degradation, and that it cannot be classified as an illness. One must measure how sharp this position was at the time. Homosexuality was considered a crime in most Western countries. It was treated by medical institutions as a sexual perversion in the psychiatric sense of the term, that is, as a pathology calling for treatment. And Freud, absolutely against the current, refuses this classification.

It is exactly the same gesture he had made decades earlier regarding hysteria — by removing it from the category of madness where the psychiatry of his time had relegated it, and by showing that it was a subjective formation that could be heard, spoken, elaborated. Freud did not pathologize. Freud *depathologized*, against his era.

And one must go further. Freud did not only refuse pathology — he directly listened to homosexuality in what it articulates in some of the greatest subjects in the history of art. His essay on **Leonardo da Vinci** (1910), then his work on **Michelangelo**, are precise analyses of the homosexual position as it articulated itself in these men, and of how it became tied to a body of work. Freud does not say: *these men were sick*. He does not say either: *these men were born that way and there is nothing to say*. He does something much rarer and much more respectful: he listens to what their history articulates, he unfolds the knots between the subject's position, his mother, his father, his identifications, his work. He gives them, by his very listening, the dignity of having a destiny that thought can illuminate.

That is the analytic gesture. And it is what contemporary right-thinking, in its determination to "protect" homosexual subjects by forbidding speech about them, refuses them today.

## Homosexuality Is a Symptom — So What?

We must then hear this, without detours.

*Yes, homosexuality is a symptom.* Like any sexual orientation. Like heterosexuality. Like any position of the subject in the sexual relation.

This statement loses all pathologizing character the moment it is correctly understood. In psychoanalysis, the symptom is not the illness. It is the formation by which a subject articulates their truth — often unbeknownst to themselves. Every subject has symptoms. The cure does not aim to eradicate them but to make them intelligible, to allow the subject to know something of them, and possibly to arrange themselves with them differently.

Sexuality is the place where symptoms condense. For a structural reason: sexuality is the locus of the *relation between the sexes*, that is, the relation to the other in what is most radical, most opaque, most traversed by the unconscious. Everything that makes symptom for a subject — their history, their identifications, their traumas, their relation to castration, their relation to the Name-of-the-Father — comes to make a sign in sexuality. It is mechanical. It applies to everyone.

To say that homosexuality is a symptom is therefore not to mark it as special or as deviant. It is to apply to it the general rule. And it is, against the right-thinking discourse that wants to make it untouchable, to give it back the dignity of being something that can be thought, elaborated, put to work. Like everything else.

## Perversion as "Father-Version"

We must now say something precise about what perversion designates in Lacanian clinical practice, because the word has become, in everyday language, more or less unusable. *Pervert* in the street means: someone who does strange things in bed. That is not at all what the word means in analysis.

Perversion, in Lacan, is a *clinical structure* — on the same level as neurosis and psychosis. It does not designate a sexual practice. It does not designate an orientation. It designates a mode of the subject's articulation to law and desire, which is constituted very early in the subject's history and which remains, thereafter, their particular way of being in the world.

In *Seminar XXIII (The Sinthome)*, Lacan proposes a reading that illuminates what perversion really carries. He plays on the homophony in French between *perversion* and **père-version** (father-version): perversion would be a certain *version of the father*. A particular way of relating to the paternal function — that is, to what, in the structure of the subject, carries the law, the limit, the prohibition, castration. The pervert has a certain way of arranging themselves with the symbolic father. They do not deny it as the psychotic does. They do not repress it as the neurotic does. They make something else of it, which passes through disavowal — they know, and they do not know, and they organize a staging that sutures the division.

What must be heard is that perversion as structure presupposes a certain relation to the symbolic father. Not to the real father — to the father as function, as principle of prohibition and order in the structure. The pervert has to do with the father, intimately, structurally. Their position is not outside the law, it is arranged *with* the law in a particular way.

Now, our era has this particularity of having made the **rejection of "patriarchy"** one of its most structuring slogans. This rejection, in its militant political form, never distinguishes between the real father — who may indeed have abused his power in this or that historical configuration — and the symbolic father, who is a structural function without which no subject is constituted. In the confusion, everything is thrown out: the abusive father and the father as function of order. And we end up in a world where no one can think serenely anymore about what the paternal function is, because the mere mention of the word "father" has become politically suspect.

This gives the Lacanian reading — *perversion as father-version* — a particularly pointed coloration today. Because what this word suggests is that perversion, far from being an excess of paternal law (as the anti-patriarchal rhetoric tends to suggest), is precisely a *version* of the relation to the father. And one does not rid oneself of perversion by ridding oneself of the father: one produces, on the contrary, new configurations of it, which one no longer knows how to recognize because one has lost the tools to think them.

This does not mean — and one must be very clear about this — that rejecting patriarchy *creates* perversion. It means that one cannot think the question of perversion seriously, nor for that matter the question of desire in general, without holding together the symbolic father and what subjects make of it. And that to give up this tool in the name of a political battle is to deprive oneself of a good half of what would need to be understood.

## There Is Not One Homosexuality

Lacan stated, in *Seminar XX*, that *The Woman does not exist*. What must be heard by this is not a provocation, but a logical thesis: the category "The Woman", as a universal encompassing feminine subjects, has no consistency. There are women, one by one, each in their singular relation to jouissance, to love, to lack.

The same logic applies, word for word, to homosexuality. **There is not one homosexuality.** There are homosexual subjects, each in their singular relation to desire, to the object, to the Other. This plurality is what clinical practice constantly encounters. And it is precisely this plurality that must prevent any global judgment on "homosexuality" as a category.

This means — and it is a consequence one must assume — that depending on configurations, homosexuality can articulate very different things for the subject. For some, it is the outcome of a history that poses no particular problem, that has tied itself to a happy speech, to a sublimation, to a rich life of desire. For others, it articulates a detour, an avoidance, a compromise with something more difficult. And yes, in certain configurations, it can articulate something that touches on perversion as structure — not systematically, not for all, but what can be true for some must remain thinkable in order for them to be heard.

What must disappear is the idea that one could answer the question "what is homosexuality?" with a general thesis. This question has no general answer. It has as many answers as there are subjects, and each answer is a history.

## The Case of Dora and Lacanian Dialectization

The most precious theoretical moment for thinking these questions is probably Lacan's rereading of the case of Dora. Freud, in his text of 1905, follows the young woman's account and sees in it, classically, a heterosexual desire for Mr. K — the friend of her father who had made advances to her. Dora resists this interpretation, the analysis fails, Freud wonders what didn't work.

Lacan takes up this case and proposes a decisive *dialectization*: what Freud had taken for a heterosexual desire for Mr. K was in reality a desire addressed to *Mrs. K*. Dora loved Mrs. K — and Mr. K only served as an indicator, as a *compass* pointing toward the place where the true object of desire was located. The detour through the man was a way of designating the woman.

This structure illuminates something essential about the **hysteric** position. In hysteria, the subject is marked by the castration complex, and they seek desire as what is found *in front of them* — in the other, in the one they take as bearer of the phallus. This is why the hysteric subject, even when their desire is addressed to another woman, can pass through the detour of the man: the man serves to *indicate where it is*, to point to the place of desire, to give the direction. Without this compass, desire remains opaque for the hysteric subject.

And this play is also found in the clinic of female homosexuality itself. Many women who love women remain traversed by the question of the castration complex and by the symbolic detour through the masculine position. A phrase one often hears in clinical practice testifies to this: *I would like to have her body*. This phrase has two meanings, and its ambiguity is what makes it precious. *To have her body in place of one's own*: identification with the desired woman, desire to be her. *To have her body against one's own*: desire in the proper sense, encounter of bodies. The phrase holds the two together — and it is in this in-between that what is at play for the subject is decided, which cannot be reduced to any generality about "female homosexuality."

## Why Communitarianism Abolishes Subjectivity

A word must be said about contemporary identity movements, because they have become the main machine for preventing the subjects concerned from elaborating their own position.

All these movements — whatever their apparent cause — function in the same mode. *They banish self-questioning.* They offer the subject a ready-made self, a solid identity, a belonging that dispenses with the work of subjectivation. They are the love of the in-group for the hatred of the other. And the most dangerous of them are those that present themselves as inclusive and progressive — wearing white gloves the better to keep their hands dirty underneath.

The subject who falls in line with the community finds an immediate relief. And this relief is precisely the problem: it *relieves* them enough to be able to keep suffering quietly, without ever being obliged to question what really makes them suffer. Community relief is not a cure, it is an anesthetic that allows suffering to persist without having to be worked through.

This operation is structurally narcissistic. The subject seeks in the same their reflection and their validation, they fix their identity in a closed image. And narcissism, as developed elsewhere, mobilizes an economy that does not make for life — see our article [Loving Oneself Before Loving Others, or Spending One's Time Putting Oneself Down: Narcissism](/articles/loving-oneself-before-others-narcissism).

What is lost in this operation is precisely what could save the subject: the possibility that their case is singular, that their history is not reducible to that of their group, that their suffering has something to say *about them* and not only about the general condition attributed to them. When homosexuality becomes "LGBT identity," it ceases to be what it is in each case — a precious subjective articulation, a rich symbolic formation, a knot of history — to become a belonging without content.

## To Conclude: The Dignity of the Subject Is Being Able to Think What Concerns Them

The title of this article says *not all homosexuals are perverts*. This formula announces less a defense than an *opening*. It says: the question can be asked. It can be asked with rigor, without prejudice, without demagoguery, and every subject has the right — which is denied to them today from all sides — to think what concerns them.

The discourse that claims to protect homosexual subjects by forbidding any questioning of their position does not protect them. It *dispossesses* them. It tells them: keep silent about what runs through you, content yourself with the identity attributed to you, do not go further than what the community validates. It is the same operation as the pathology of old, formulated in reverse: a refusal of singularity, an erasure of the subject, an assignment to a category.

Psychoanalysis proposes something else. It proposes to the subject — whoever they may be, in whatever position they find themselves — the possibility of elaborating their own truth. Not a collective truth, not a community truth, not a prefabricated truth. *Their truth, theirs, in their singular history.* It is demanding. It is uncomfortable. It is, sometimes, painful. It is also the only thing that frees.

To think is not to accuse. To question is not to pathologize. And to refuse the subject the right to think what they are, what they do, what they desire, under the pretext of defending them, is the worst of dispossessions.
